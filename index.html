Background

Purdue University houses more than 50,000 students each year. With limited on-campus apartments and dormitories, students highly consider moving to off-campus properties.

Off-campus housing is just as hard to aquire and comes with a strenuous search process

In 2023 alone, students have camped overnight outside of property managment companies just for a shot at securing housing.

When searching for housing, sources of information lack consistency and comprehensive details, leaving students unsure about the reliability and suitability of their housing choices.

Students camping overnight for a chance at signing a lease. Read More

The Challenge

How Might We provide Purdue students, especially those who are new the search process, with accessible guidance and a centralized, dependable source of housing information generated from community discussion?

How Might We alleviate the challenges associated with making housing decisions based on limited, potentially biased, and inconsistent sources, such as word-of-mouth?

Final Walkthrough
Limitations of Current Design

Contacting management teams and landlords to bring their perspectives to this project was challenging. Therefore, our website is more designed around the perspective of Purdue students only. However, we cannot deny the interest the housing management teams would have in our design as this will directly affect students' perception of their properties and business in general. In the future, it would be beneficial to get information from this stakeholder to design a safe space for both stakeholders. Housing should be a win-win situation for students and landlords. Therefore, designing with both communities' perspectives would further improve our design

Managerial Motivation to use our website: The above discussion leads to the lack of motivation for management companies to use our website. Since our intention is to strengthen students' voices, we hope this website will inform stakeholders (housing companies and relevant stakeholders) to become aware of the situations Purdue students have to go through and may come up with responsive plans.

Next Steps

Content Validity: We focused heavily on user authentication and verification, but we did not have the time to devise solutions to improve the quality of information on a community-based website like ours. In our next steps, to ensure the accuracy of information, we would like to come up with ideas to fact-check information and photos uploaded by management. This would be important for those who have a stake in preserving a brand image, which could lead to misinformation, which our project is focused on minimizing. To tacke this, managerial research would be important for us to conduct so that we can understand their perspective and taiolor our solutions to them as well.

Student's stake: We would also want to brainstorm how to incentiviize students to post questions and especially answer them on the forum. We briefly discussed this in a peer feedback session, but to get a more solid idea, we would benefit from conducting another round of user research where students can share their opinion on the platform, which features they find useful, and how they might be prompted to engage more with the website.

Background

Mic is a start-up concept focused on empowering CASAs (Court Appointed Special Advocates). CASAs are a trained community of volunteers who advocate for abused and neglected children in the child welfare system. CASAs regurally meet with the child (or siblings) and provide the best recommendation for their well-being on behalf of the child in court.

Indiana volunteers represented over 23,290 abused and neglected children in 2021 alone. Volunteers manage dozens of relationships with therapists, foster parents, child services, teachers, and many more with no dedicated communication channel when speaking on highly sensitive information.

The journey of a CASA volunteer

Probelm Statement:

CASA volunteers with full-time jobs need a system to take, organize, review, upload meeting notes and information, enabling them to provide the best recommendations and insights when writing court reports.

Project Overview

Key Activities

M1: Framework Development

Leading Question: What are the most important aspects of a CASA's journey within a case timeline?

The primary goals throughout this phase of the design process were:

  • Generate data to drive insight creation and assist in the decision-making process.
  • Hear real stories from stakeholders to gain empathy and identify the most important problem to solve.
  • Create visualizatons of the problem space to facilitate a shared understanding of CASA experiences.

In order to achieve this we created 4 research questions to guide our framework development:

From background research and interviews with CASA's, we developed a diagram to showcase the journey a CASA goes through for each case.

After answering the other questions and narrowing down our scope, we decided to focus on Phase 2 and 3 of a CASA's journey because it is where we identified the most difficulty for CASAs.

We then identified the stakeholders CASAs have to keep regular communication with during a case. These different contacts all provide insights on the well-being of the child. Therefore, having secure channels and documented communication is important for CASAs when advocating for the child.

Each CASA volunteer’s case is a unique circumstance, but there are general tasks and responsibilities that all CASA’s face. The main responsibility of a CASA volunteer is to advocate for the child’s best interest while maintaining confidentiality. To achieve this goal, CASA volunteers must understand the situation, support the child’s needs, and represent the child by:

CASA volunteers, despite unique case circumstances, work to advocate for the child’s best interest while maintaining confidentiality. In addition to addressing the child’s needs, this section explores how CASAs have informational needs (case data) and emotional needs (breaks between cases to recuperate) to effectively fulfill their responsibilities.

How did we answer these questions? Interviews with users and stakeholders

We reached out to different stakeholders through personal connections, reddit posts, and contacts from our sponor and were able to conduct interviews with 3 CASA volunters, 1 CASA supervisor, 1 attorney, and 1 CPS worker. While we hoped to hear many different perspectives, we faced a lot of difficulty in finding volunteers who were willing to speak with us (privacy is an important aspect of being a CASA). We were able to gather a lot of information from the interviews we did conduct, but we were unable to gather as much information as we had hoped.

After we conducted the interviews, we created an affinity diagram of the insights.

M2: Visualizing the Solution

Leading Question: Which critical insights can we use to inform design and empower CASAs?

The primary goals throughout this phase of the design process were:

  • Identify and prioritize CASA needs to understand the best areas the team can make an impact.
  • Generate low-fidelity mockups for solutions that address pain points, empower volunteers, and allow the opportunity for solution iteration.
  • Communicate solutions in a way that is easily understandable by the sponsor and team.

Codesign Workshop

In order to narrow down our scope and identify the most important needs to solve for, we completed a codesign workshop with current CASA volunteers to design with them. Throughout this workshop, we aimed to increase understanding of what tasks CASAs find the most valuable and the time-consuming. Next, we had each CASA identify the top 3 needs they find the most important to improving CASA's experience. Read more about our codesign here.

Organizing Needs and Features

Inspired by many ideas from the workshops, the team split into groups to focus on the three main needs of CASA volunteers identified through our codesign. Each group sketched ideas for their specific need. We discussed our designs with each other and iterated on these sketches 3 times to better visualize our design concepts before transforming them to mid-fidelity screens. We then created our vision document through a visually annotated document to send to our sponsor to present and narrow down to our potential final solutions. View our vision document here.

M3: Prototyping

Leading Question: How might we create a system to effectively take, organize, review, upload meeting notes and information, enabling CASAs to provide the best recommendations and insights when writing court reports?

The primary goals throughout this phase of the design process were:

  • Build a comprehensive functional prototype based on the prioritized needs of CASAs, ensuring it incorporates key features and addresses identified pain points.

Final Design Walkthrough

Our team developed mid-fidelity prototypes of a mobile and web ecosystem for CASAs to upload and organize their notes and information to help them prepare for court.

Our work represents a comprehensive and innovative approach to empowering CASAs with needed solutions at their fingertips. By developing these solutions, we hope to increase efficiency and make CASA responsibilities more manageable for full-time employees who are CASAs.

IoT and Rapid Prototyping

The interconnection of physical devices and services is becoming increasingly prevalent in today’s networked world. Numerous projections indicate that the ‘Internet of Things’ will become commonplace in users’ everyday lives in the coming decades. As a result, UX designers need to have appropriate knowledge and skills to design for multiple devices and services that work coherently and lead to holistic experiences. Although UX designers are not typically trained as developers or data scientists, having basic programming and data-related skills can be highly beneficial. Such skills empower UX designers to quickly create mockups and prototypes to test design ideas.

Prototyping Process

The initial idea for a prototype that helps users learn music theory or an instrument came from an interest in the experience of learning an instrument. We first began by looking into existing technology that involves getting live feedback when playing along to different songs. While there are a lot of existing apps and games available that help with learning music notes, there aren’t physical devices that people can use to practice playing music besides having the physical instrument themselves. This results in a lack of access to music devices for those wanting to learn, as access to instruments often requires expensive equipment that beginners may be reluctant to invest in without knowing if it’ll even pay off.

Our goal was to create a solution that allows users to practice and develop their musical knowledge without having to spend a lot of money to practice and acquire equipment as a beginner. Not only this, but getting tutors or private teachers to learn music can be expensive. This device would still help someone effectively learn notes without having to spend lots of money. We also aimed to make the learning experience enjoyable through gamification and provide users a goal to work toward.

We used tinkercad to help us understand the circuit and verify we have the correct setup

After completing the sketch of the circuit, we then used it to help build the circuit using a buzzer, breadboard, an arduino uno, wires, and buttons.

Arduino IDE code uploaded to the arduino UNO. The conditional statements enable the breadboard to act as a piano.

After uploading this code to the arduino, we began to develop the p5.js code to enable serial communication through a live server. This will allow users to plug in their arduino breadboard piano to their computer and use live serial communication to play an interactive musical note learning game.

Initial interface made with p5.js - Accepts user input through serial communication

Once I understood the flow of how the game would progress, I developed and refind the p5.js code to visually present feedback based on user input through the interface. Read more about the p5.js code below:

The ‘draw’ function enables the images and text to change based on user input. We used two index values for the note images on the left side and the game images on the right side to allow the images to change depending on the step of the game

The switch statement is the main control of the game. The switch function has 8 different cases: a case for each step of the game that calls the corresponding game function and the ending of the game. The switch statement uses the gameIndex to determine which case the user is on and increments by 1 each step. The only value passed through each function is the value the user inputs into the arduino keyboard.

There are 8 different musicGame functions, where each function uses 2 if-conditional statements to give feedback and checks to see if the user inputs both notes correctly. The if statements also change the different messages on the screen based on user input.
